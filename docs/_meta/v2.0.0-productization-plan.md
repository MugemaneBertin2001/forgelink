# ForgeLink v2.0.0 — Productization Plan

**Date prepared:** 2026-04-22
**Target release:** v2.0.0 (no earlier than 2026-10-14; six months out)
**Preceding release:** v1.1.0 (six weeks out; public-worthy demo checkpoint)
**Budget assumption:** 60 hours/month solo, degrading to 40 hours/month under day-job + Upwork + Copa pressure.

## 0. Framing and version name

The retrospective diagnosed v1.0.0 honestly: a scaffold with multiple subsystems deployed-but-not-wired. Honest labelling was the right v1.0 answer.

The v2.0.0 answer, post-review, is a **scaffold-as-first-class-architecture** model. The architecture's breadth is the credibility story. We do not strengthen the product by cutting surface area; we strengthen it by landing the wired core, keeping the scaffolded surface visibly labelled as planned, and shipping enough running evidence that a reviewer trusts the planned labels.

The bar for v2.0.0 is that every claim in the README and in the architecture docs is either (a) backed by running behavior a reviewer can observe within ten minutes, or (b) explicitly labelled *planned* with the retained scaffold visible in the repo and a release target in ROADMAP.md. We do not hide the gap; we do not overstate; we do not delete optionality.

This is a tradeoff, not a free choice:

- **What it earns:** reviewers who value architectural breadth (senior architects, platform reviewers, IIoT buyers evaluating a reference architecture) see a complete platform story with visible roadmap discipline. Optionality is preserved for future engagements that may ask for SPIFFE, AMQP, object storage, GitOps, predictive maintenance, etc.
- **What it costs:** skeptical reviewers (CTOs in due-diligence mode) will poke at every *planned* label and ask how much is real. The honesty discipline in zero-trust.md and the productization plan is the mitigation — labels must be precise enough that the poking is fast and the answer is always defensible.

**Version name: v2.0.0 (not v1.2.0 or v1.5.0).** Justified because this cycle introduces breaking changes:

- NetworkPolicy allow-list enforcement breaks any deploy workflow that assumed flat intra-namespace reachability.
- Service-to-service mTLS (M2, via cert-manager-issued internal PKI) breaks clients that do not present a cert.
- Observability, demo deployment, and CI gating tighten materially.

The feature surface is additive at v2.0.0 (no deletions), but the operational posture changes enough to warrant the major bump.

### Security identity decision — cert-manager PKI as v2.0.0 realized path; SPIRE retained as scaffold

The v2.0.0 mTLS claim is backed by cert-manager-issued workload certs from an internal ClusterIssuer (CA type). This delivers observable mTLS (Spring access logs show client DN; cleartext probes fail at handshake) within the 60 hr/month budget.

SPIRE manifests are retained in the repo under `k8s/base/spire/` and `k8s/helm/spire/` with their v1.0.0 state preserved. zero-trust.md describes them honestly as *planned SPIFFE workload identity, deployed-not-wired, v2.2.0+ target*. They are not deployed to the OCI demo (to stay within resource budget), but they ship with the repo for any operator who wants to stand them up.

Why keep both paths:

- **cert-manager PKI is the productization path.** Demo-visible, achievable in budget, covers the mTLS claim for v2.0.0.
- **SPIRE remains the identity-substrate option.** Consulting engagements that ask for SPIFFE specifically (e.g., multi-cluster trust domain federation, third-party workload attestation) can build on the committed manifests rather than starting from scratch.

**Execution rule:** `docs/architecture/zero-trust.md` is rewritten in M1 to match this split. SPIRE sections move to a "Planned identity substrate" subsection labelled clearly; the mTLS section describes the cert-manager-issued approach as the realized path for v2.0.0. Updates to zero-trust.md land in the same commit that introduces the cert-manager PKI manifests (M2 start), not as a standalone doc change.

### Hosting target — OCI Always Free, resource budget

OCI Always Free ARM limits (per account):
- **Compute:** up to 4 OCPU + 24 GB RAM across A1.Flex instances.
- **Block storage:** 200 GB total.
- **Load Balancer:** 1 flexible (10 Mbps) free.
- **Object Storage:** 20 GB free.

Per-service resource budget for the full stack. Three columns per dimension: `req` (Kubernetes resources.requests, guaranteed minimum), `limit` (resources.limits, burst ceiling), and `bursty?` (whether this service is likely to consume toward the limit under load, not just at steady state).

OCI Always Free ceiling: 4 OCPU + 24 GB RAM total.

**CPU headroom analysis:**
- Requests (4.30) exceed the 4 OCPU ceiling by 7.5%. This is sustainable because Kubernetes requests are guaranteed floors; actual steady-state utilization on this workload is ~15–20% aggregate.
- Limit totals (13.75) are nominal burst capacity; the scheduler will oversubscribe aggressively. Under genuine concurrent bursts, CPU throttling occurs in that order: Prometheus → Celery → Django. Acceptable — none of those are demo-critical-path.

**RAM headroom analysis:**
- Requests (13.1 GB) leave 10.9 GB below the 24 GB ceiling. Comfortable at steady state.
- Limits (20.1 GB) leave 3.9 GB of the 24 GB ceiling for kernel, systemd, k3s overhead, and burst headroom. Tight but above the user-specified 20 GB threshold for the "OCI-only" verdict.
- Bursty services (Kafka, TDengine, Postgres, Prometheus) account for 11.5 GB of the 20.1 GB limit total — if two burst concurrently at max limit (e.g., a telemetry spike pushing Kafka + TDengine together), the cluster still has headroom, but memory pressure is the first warning signal.

**Verdict:** Full stack fits OCI Always Free at requests. At limits, we are within the ceiling but with the warning that simultaneous max-burst across all four bursty services is not accommodated. This is acceptable for a steady demo workload but not for a sustained load test.

**Local-only fallback (if OCI budget becomes insufficient):**

If a future scope addition (e.g., Loki in v2.1.0, or OTel in v2.2.0) pushes totals past 20 GB, the following services move to `docker-compose.yml` only (for reviewer clone-and-run) and are not OCI-resident:

1. **Prometheus + Grafana** — lowest demo-value-per-GB. A reviewer can point their browser at local Grafana when cloning; the hosted demo continues serving the core UI without observability.
2. **cert-manager** — moves to manual cert issuance on the hosted demo if needed (retain on local).
3. **Last resort — opcua-simulator + edge-gateway** — run only in local docker-compose mode, with the hosted demo using a pre-seeded static TDengine snapshot. This removes live telemetry from the demo and is the last fallback before cutting paid-tier compute.

No service moves to OCI paid tier without explicit scope approval.

**Load-test budget note:** Performance characterization in M3 runs on a separate OCI VM (can be paid tier for a one-week window, estimated ~$15–30 one-time cost) so the public demo is not impacted. Demo continues on Always Free throughout.

## 5. Branching strategy

**Hybrid: short-lived topic branches off main, tags cut from main, no long-lived feature branch.**

Justification:
- `main` must remain releasable. A six-month feature branch accrues merge conflicts a solo engineer cannot afford.
- Each work item in Section 2 is independently shippable: cert-manager alone is a complete change; NetworkPolicy allow-list is a complete change.
- Topic branches have 3–7 day lifetimes. Each merges to main after CI green + manual smoke test on the OCI demo (which redeploys from main automatically once CD lands).

Topic branch names:
- `feat/scaffold-labels` — scaffold-label reconciliation (no deletions), single PR
- `feat/cert-manager` — M1
- `feat/networkpolicy-allowlist` — M1
- `feat/observability-k8s` — M1
- `feat/oci-demo-deploy` — M1
- `feat/mobile-apk-release` — M1
- `feat/emqx-tls` — M2
- `feat/mtls-internal-pki` — M2
- `feat/notification-email` — M2
- `feat/performance-characterization` — M3
- `feat/runbooks-and-adrs` — M3
- `feat/demo-polish` — M4
- `chore/rewrite-docs-present-tense` — M4

Exception: the scaffold-labels branch touches many docs and benefits from a single careful review pass. Kept small by being strictly additive (label copy; no code or manifest changes).

### "Releasable on main" — the enforceable checklist

The constraint "main must remain releasable" is enforced, not aspirational. Before merging any topic branch, every item below must be true on the resulting main state. A pre-merge smoke check runs through this list:

1. **`docker compose up -d` completes and stays healthy.** All advertised services reach their healthcheck green state within 3 minutes on a clean laptop. No CrashLoopBackOff, no dependency failures.
2. **End-to-end smoke holds.** Seed the simulator, POST a test telemetry record, verify it lands in TDengine via the Kafka consumer path. Trigger a test alert, verify it hits Slack (or the demo sink).
3. **The v1.0.0 git tag still builds.** `git checkout v1.0.0 && docker compose build` succeeds. A regression in the base image or a dropped dep that breaks historical tags is a main-state regression.
4. **No broken internal links in docs.** Run the link-audit check (`scripts/check-doc-links.sh` — to be committed in M1 week 1 if not yet present). Every internal markdown link resolves.
5. **No half-landed feature branches.** Every merged branch must be complete against its own acceptance criteria; partial work is committed on its topic branch, never merged to main.
6. **CI green on the merge commit.** All four workflows (`ci`, `build`, `deploy` dry-run, `release` dry-run) pass.
7. **`scripts/deploy.sh` to a fresh local k3s completes successfully.** Demonstrates main is cluster-deployable, not just compose-runnable.

A merge that breaks any of these is rolled back with `git revert` on main; the topic branch is reopened for repair. No "forward-fix it next PR" on main.

This checklist is codified (not just described here) as `scripts/check-releasable.sh` in M1 week 1. CI runs it as a required check on merge-to-main.

Tags cut from main at M1 completion (v1.1.0) and M4 completion (v2.0.0). M2 and M3 do not tag — they land on main as-they-go.

## Appendix A — Scope clarifications and the framing-A pivot

Persistent record of clarifying questions raised during plan review and the scope pivot from a deletion strategy to framing A (scaffold-as-first-class architecture).

**A.1 `mobile/flutter-app/` — retained as legacy scaffold, labelled.** Contains a README and an empty `lib/` subdirectory; the active Flutter app lives at `services/flutter-app/` (21 `.dart` files, Riverpod + GoRouter + Dio + Socket.IO). Pre-framing-A plan called for deletion as vestigial. Post-framing-A: retained as a historical marker; `mobile/flutter-app/README.md` gets a one-line note cross-referencing the active app location. No functional impact either way.

**A.2 RabbitMQ — retained as scaffolded future-command-orchestration substrate.** The original CLAUDE.md scoped RabbitMQ for "commands and alerts." The as-built system delivers both roles through other transports:
- **Commands** flow through MQTT on a per-area `commands` subtree, enforced by the EMQX ACL (`config/emqx/acl.conf:25,31,37,43`).
- **Alert dispatch** runs on Kafka via the `alerts.notifications` topic.
- **Celery** uses Redis as broker (`apps/core/settings.py:249`).
- No AMQP client is imported anywhere in `services/`.

Pre-framing-A plan called for deletion as dead weight. Post-framing-A: retained in `docker-compose.yml` and labelled in CLAUDE.md as "planned for future command-orchestration workflows that need AMQP topic exchanges" (v2.1.0+ target). The retained scaffold preserves optionality for engagements that ask for AMQP-specific semantics.

**A.3 OPC-UA simulator + Edge Gateway — KEEP, demo-critical.** These are the demo's data source. Together they produce the continuous telemetry that populates every Grafana panel, every device detail page, and every alert trigger on the live demo. Demo-visible function:
- **OPC-UA simulator** — exposes an OPC-UA address space mirroring the ISA-95 steel-plant hierarchy, driven by Django Simulator app via Redis pub/sub.
- **Edge Gateway** — OPC-UA client that subscribes to simulator value changes and republishes onto the MQTT UNS topics.

Both run on the OCI demo 24/7. No scope change across framing pivots.

**A.4 Framing pivot (2026-04-23): delete list dropped.** Initial plan version (commit `ab26e67`) and its adjustments (`e279fa3`) prescribed a delete list covering `slack-bot/`, `apps/ai/`, `mobile/flutter-app/`, MinIO, RabbitMQ, SPIRE manifests, `k8s/argocd/`, Vault references, and Predictive Maintenance language. On review, user chose framing A: scaffold-as-first-class-architecture. Rationale: architectural breadth is itself the credibility story; optionality for future engagements (SPIFFE, AMQP, object storage, GitOps, ML) is preserved by keeping the scaffolds with honest labels rather than removing them. The tradeoff (see §0): reviewers who value breadth gain a complete platform story; reviewers in due-diligence mode will poke at planned labels and require the honesty discipline to hold. This appendix entry documents the pivot so future plan readers do not reconstruct it from commit archaeology.
